How eBay Final Value Fees (FVF) Work

eBay primarily makes money through the Final Value Fee. Unlike some platforms that only charge a fee on the item price, eBay calculates its percentage based on the total amount the buyer pays. This includes the item price, shipping costs, and even sales tax (in the US) or VAT (in the UK).

For most casual sellers in the USA, this rate is 13.25% plus a $0.30 fixed fee. In the UK, the standard rate is often 12.8% plus £0.30. However, these rates fluctuate based on the category. For instance, high-value electronics often have lower percentage caps, while clothing might have higher rates but lower listing fees. Using an ebay profit calculator ensures you aren't surprised by these variations.

The Impact of eBay Stores and Top Rated Status

If you are a high-volume seller, opening an eBay Store can significantly lower your overhead. Store subscribers often get a reduced FVF rate (sometimes as low as 4-9% for specific categories) and a higher number of free monthly listings.

Furthermore, achieving "Top Rated Seller" status isn't just about the badge; it offers a 10% discount on your final value fees for listings that offer 30-day returns and 1-day handling. Our tool allows you to toggle these settings to see exactly how much money you save by maintaining high performance standards.

Hidden Costs: Promoted Listings and Shipping

In today's competitive marketplace, many sellers use "Promoted Listings" to boost visibility. This is an additional percentage fee you agree to pay eBay if a buyer clicks your ad and buys the item. It’s a powerful tool, but it eats directly into your profit margin.

Don't forget shipping. Even if you offer "Free Shipping," you are still paying for the label. A common mistake is forgetting that eBay takes a cut of the shipping money you collect from the buyer. Our calculator helps you factor in both the shipping you charge and the actual cost of the label to ensure your ROI remains healthy.
