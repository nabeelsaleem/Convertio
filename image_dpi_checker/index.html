The Print vs. Digital Myth: What Actually is DPI?

Before you check dpi of image assets, you must understand what the acronym actually means. DPI stands for "Dots Per Inch." It is strictly a physical printing term. It tells a commercial inkjet or laser printer how many physical dots of ink to spray onto a single square inch of paper.

If someone asks you how to check image dpi for a website, they are technically asking the wrong question. Screens use PPI (Pixels Per Inch). A digital image measuring 1000x1000 pixels will display exactly the same on a monitor whether its internal DPI metadata is set to 72 or 300. The DPI tag only activates and matters the second that file is sent to a physical printing press.

However, the industry uses the terms interchangeably. When a magazine publisher demands a "300 DPI high-res file," they are dictating print density. If you supply a 72 DPI image, the printer spreads the available pixels too thin across the physical paper, resulting in a blurry catastrophe. Using an image resolution checker is your first line of defense to ensure your density math is correct before the ink hits the canvas.

The 300 DPI Golden Rule: 300 Dots Per Inch is the absolute minimum standard for high-quality, professional offset printing (magazines, brochures, business cards).

Large Format Exceptions: For massive billboards viewed from highway distances, 72 DPI or even 30 DPI is perfectly acceptable because the human eye blends the dots from afar.

Why you need it: You cannot guess density by looking at a screen. You must use a dedicated dpi checker image utility to read the raw mathematical tag.

Why Native Operating Systems Fail You

Many clients ask me how to tell dpi of image files without downloading third-party software. On Windows, you can sometimes right-click, select Properties, and check the Details tab. On a Mac, you can use the Inspector in Preview.
