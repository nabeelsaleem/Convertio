How to Calculate Freelance Hourly Rate the Right Way

One of the biggest mistakes new freelancers make is taking their previous full-time salary and dividing it by 2,080 (the standard number of work hours in a year). If you do this, you will effectively be taking a massive pay cut. Unlike an employee, you have to pay for your own health insurance, software subscriptions, self-employment taxes, and retirement savings. Our tool simplifies this process by helping you understand how to calculate freelance hourly rate based on your actual financial needs, not just a raw salary number.

The Truth About the Freelance Salary to Hourly Converter

A simple freelance salary to hourly converter often lies to you because it assumes you are working 40 billable hours a week. In reality, no freelancer bills 100% of their time. You have administrative tasks, marketing, client meetings, and vacation days that are unpaid.

To convert a salary to a freelance rate accurately, you must factor in "overhead." I usually recommend the "Rule of Thirds": one-third for taxes, one-third for expenses, and one-third for your take-home pay. This tool handles that math for you instantly so you can see exactly what you need to charge to net your target income.

Using the Minimum Billable Hours Calculator

Capacity planning is the hardest part of scaling a freelance business. You might want to earn $100,000, but if you can only bill 20 hours a week due to other commitments, your rate needs to be significantly higher. This tool acts as a minimum billable hours calculator. By inputting your target income and your estimated weekly availability, we work backward to tell you the floor price you cannot go below.

Tips for Setting Your Rate

1. Don't ignore taxes: Self-employment tax can take a huge bite out of your earnings. Always aim higher to create a buffer.
2. Account for dry spells: Freelancing is feast or famine. Your hourly rate should be high enough to help you save for the months when clients are quiet.
3. Value-based pricing: Once you know your minimum hourly rate from this calculator, consider it your "floor." If the project brings high value to the client, feel free to charge more.
4. Re-evaluate annually: As your skills improve and inflation rises, your rate should go up. Run this calculation at least once a year.
