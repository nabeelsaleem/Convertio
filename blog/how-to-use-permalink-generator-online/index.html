What exactly is a permalink generator?

Basically, it’s a tool that takes a messy headline or a long string of text and turns it into a "slug." That’s the part of the URL that comes after your domain name. A good permalink generator doesn't just swap spaces for dashes; it strips out "stop words" (like a, an, the, with) and prepares the link for maximum search engine visibility.

Here's the thing: search engines prefer URLs that are descriptive but concise. If your URL is too long, it gets truncated in the search results. That looks bad. It looks unprofessional. And worst of all? It lowers your click-through rate. Using the best permalink generator 2026 edition means you're following the latest standards for URL length and readability.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

I’ve made plenty of mistakes in my career. Back in 2018, I once migrated a whole site and forgot to use a consistent permalink generator. The result? A mess of underscores and hyphens that took me weeks to fix with 301 redirects. Don't be like 2018-me.

1. Using Underscores instead of Hyphens

Google treats hyphens (-) as spaces, but it treats underscores (_) as joiners. This means seo_tips is seen as one word "seotips," while seo-tips is seen as "seo tips." Always use hyphens. Our tool does this by default, so you don't even have to think about it.

2. Leaving Dates in the URL

Unless you're a major news site, putting /2026/02/14/ in your URL is a bad move. It makes your content look dated the moment 2027 rolls around. Use a clean, evergreen slug instead. If you need to check if your page is getting too heavy with all these changes, try our page size checker.

3. Forgetting to Update Internal Links

If you change a permalink after a page is live, you must set up a redirect. Otherwise, you'll end up with a 404 error. I always recommend using our csv validator if you're doing this at scale for a large e-commerce site to ensure your redirect maps are clean.
