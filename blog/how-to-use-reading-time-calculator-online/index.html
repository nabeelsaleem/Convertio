How to Use Reading Time Calculator: A Quick Tutorial

Getting this right isn't rocket science, but there are a few tricks to make your data more accurate. Here is my personal reading time calculator tutorial for getting the best results:

  1. Clean your text: Copy your content from Google Docs or Word, but try to leave out the random comments or "Note to self" bits at the bottom.
  2. Paste into the tool: Head over to the reading time calculator and drop your text into the main box.
  3. Set your WPM: If your audience is mostly experts (think developers or doctors), drop the WPM to 180. If it's a breezy lifestyle blog, 250 is fine.
  4. Check the Speaking Time: If you're planning to turn this into a video or a voiceover, pay close attention to the verbal estimate. It's almost always longer than the silent reading time.

Pro Tip: Don't Forget the Images!

Most tools only count words. But humans take about 12 seconds to "read" an image or a complex chart. If you have 10 images in your post, add 2 minutes to whatever the reading time calculator tells you. While you're at it, make sure your images aren't wonky by using an image aspect ratio calculator.

Frequently Asked Questions

How accurate is a reading time calculator? ▶

It's very accurate for average word counts, but it can't account for how many times a reader stops to look at a photo or clicks a link. Most use a standard 200-250 WPM formula which covers about 80% of the population.

What is the average words per minute (WPM) for adults? ▶

Most adults read silently at 238 words per minute for non-fiction. For fiction, it usually jumps up because the narrative flow is easier to follow. Our tool lets you adjust this to fit your specific content type.

Should I include the time for images in my estimate? ▶

Yes! A good rule of thumb is to add 10-12 seconds per image. If you have a lot of charts or complex diagrams, you might even add 30 seconds per graphic to be safe.

Can I use this for speech writing? ▶

Absolutely. Just make sure to look at the speaking time result, not the silent reading time. People speak much slower than they read—usually around 130-150 words per minute.
